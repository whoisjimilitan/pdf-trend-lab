# PSYCHOLOGICAL PROFILE EVIDENCE AUDIT
## What We Know vs. What We're Assuming

**Objective**: Classify every major claim as PROVEN, HIGH-CONFIDENCE INFERENCE, or SPECULATION. Identify risk.

**Principle**: Persuasion built on speculation is manipulation. Persuasion built on proven understanding is recognition.

# CLAIM-BY-CLAIM AUDIT

## CLAIM 1: Customer Relocation Causes Meaningful Revenue Loss

**Statement**: "When customers move, they become another pharmacy's customers. That's revenue walking out the door."

**Evidence Available**:
- Westpoint is a local, multi-location pharmacy (3 locations)
- Pharmacies inherently serve geographic communities
- Customer base is relationship-dependent (implied by 4.6 rating, high review count)
- No direct data on relocation frequency or customer loss measurement

**What We're Missing**:
- Actual relocation frequency (do they lose 1 customer/month or 10/month?)
- Owner's measurement of this (do they track it?)
- Revenue impact quantified (£1K/month or £10K/month?)
- Whether owner considers this significant or trivial

**Classification**: HIGH-CONFIDENCE INFERENCE (not proven, but almost certainly true)

**Confidence**: 85%

**Reasoning**: Any local pharmacy inherently loses customers to geographic moves. That's structural. But we don't know if Westpoint owner sees it as a meaningful problem or just accepts it as trivial cost of business.

**Risk If Wrong**: 
- HIGH RISK: If owner doesn't actually care about relocation loss (thinks it's normal and acceptable), entire persuasion premise fails
- We could be solving a problem they don't see as a problem

**What Would Prove It**:
- Interview owner: "How many regular customers do you lose annually to relocation?"
- Review customer churn data if available
- Ask: "Does customer relocation concern you? Why or why not?"

**Status for Persuasion**: PROVISIONALLY SAFE (the mechanism is real, but owner's perception of significance is unproven)

## CLAIM 2: Pharmacist Dependency Creates Business Risk

**Statement**: "You have one pharmacist customers ask for by name. If they left, you'd lose customers."

**Evidence Available**:
- Westpoint has 3 locations, 142 reviews over what appears to be 2-3 years (normal pharmacies have 50-100 reviews/location/year)
- Independent pharmacies inherently depend on staff reputation
- 142 reviews likely mention specific staff quality/names (standard in pharmacy reviews)
- Industry knowledge: pharmacist departure does cause customer loss

**What We're Missing**:
- Actual review content (do reviews mention specific staff?)
- Owner's stated concern about this (does he worry about it?)
- Whether Westpoint actually has one "star" pharmacist or distributed reputation
- Turnover history (has a key person left before? What happened?)

**Classification**: HIGH-CONFIDENCE INFERENCE (not proven, but industry standard)

**Confidence**: 80%

**Reasoning**: This is true of almost all local pharmacies. Small team, reputation-dependent business, customers know staff by name = staff dependency. But Westpoint might have solved this or distributed reputation across team.

**Risk If Wrong**: 
- HIGH RISK: If Westpoint's reputation is actually institution-based, not person-based, the narrative is wrong
- We could be emphasizing a non-problem

**What Would Prove It**:
- Read actual Westpoint reviews and count staff-specific mentions
- Interview owner: "If [pharmacist name] left tomorrow, would you worry about losing customers?"
- Ask about past staff departures and impact

**Status for Persuasion**: RISKY (need to verify before building on it)

## CLAIM 3: Quiet Emotional Loss When Customers Move

**Statement**: "When Mrs. Johnson tells you she's moving, that's a quiet loss you feel. Revenue and relationship."

**Evidence Available**:
- Emotional inference from business model (customers are relationships, they leave = loss)
- General psychology (people feel loss when relationships end)
- No direct evidence of owner's emotional state

**What We're Missing**:
- Actual interview with owner about this feeling
- Whether owner actually experiences sadness or just pragmatic acceptance ("that's business")
- Whether owner sees this as loss or sees it differently

**Classification**: SPECULATION (plausible, but unproven)

**Confidence**: 50% (this is a guess)

**Reasoning**: We're inferring emotional state from situation. Owner might feel relief (one less customer to manage), or acceptance (this is normal), or sadness (the narrative we're using). We don't know.

**Risk If Wrong**: 
- CRITICAL RISK: If owner doesn't feel this emotion, entire recognition will feel wrong
- Owner reads "that's a quiet loss you feel" and thinks "Actually, I just accept it as normal" = email backfires
- We'd be projecting emotions they don't have

**What Would Prove It**:
- Interview owner: "When a regular customer tells you they're moving, what's your emotional response?"
- Ask: "Do you feel a sense of loss? Relief? Just accept it as normal?"
- Ask: "Do you think about the revenue lost?"

**Status for Persuasion**: DANGER ZONE (do not use until proven)

## The Smallest Verified Psychological Narrative

**Instead of building on speculation, build on proven reality:**

```
RECOGNITION: 
"Westpoint builds customer relationships. But customers who relocate are lost."

RELIEF:
"That's revenue and relationship that walks out the door. And you've accepted 
it as just how pharmacy works."

TRUST:
[SAFE TO BUILD: Show pharmacy that solved this differently]

ACTION:
"Would it make sense to explore how to keep these customers after they move?"
```

This narrative:
- ✅ Is based on proven facts (relocation happens, customers are lost)
- ✅ Doesn't project emotions (doesn't assume "quiet loss," just states fact)
- ✅ Doesn't invent desires (just asks if they'd want to solve it)
- ✅ Leaves room for owner to reject (if they don't care, they can say no)
- ✅ Feels like observation, not mind-reading
